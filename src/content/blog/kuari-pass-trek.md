## Day 1 — Rishikesh to Pipalkoti

My first solo trekking adventure began early — three dormitory mates from Mumbai and I were headed to Pipalkoti, a quiet town in Chamoli district, Uttarakhand. The journey covers roughly 220 kilometres and takes 6–7 hours along the Alaknanda river. Somewhere along the way, you pass Devprayag, where the Alaknanda and Bhagirathi rivers merge to form the Ganges. I made a note to stop there properly on a future trip.

I'd loaded up on travel sickness tablets. The mountain roads have a cadence of their own — slow, winding, beautiful — and the views more than compensated for the discomfort.

Pipalkoti itself is understated. No tourist rush, no souvenir shops aggressively waving at you. I explored the local market, bought a pair of cheap woollen socks I suspected I'd need, and tried to quieten the low hum of first-timer nerves.

The mandatory health check came next. They measure your blood pressure before letting you proceed — mine nearly exceeded the acceptable range. Not from altitude; from anxiety. The nurse gave me a look. I took a few slow breaths and managed to squeak through.

The evening briefing introduced our trek leaders: Ira and Bunny. They walked us through equipment — microspikes, trekking poles, sleeping bag ratings — sleeping arrangements, altitude acclimatisation protocols, and what to do if someone felt unwell. There were about 25 of us. We had homemade dinner together, shared a room, and I lay awake for a while listening to the mountains be quiet in a way Mumbai never is.

## Day 2 — Pipalkoti to Gulling Top Campsite

The actual trekking began with a 45-kilometre transfer to Tugasi village. Once assembled, the group kicked things off with a chant: *"Namah Parvati Pateh, Har Har Mahadev."* It sounds like something you'd roll your eyes at in the city. Up here, at the base of a trail with ice peaks above you, it worked. The energy shifted.

The first trek was 3 kilometres uphill, taking around 3 hours. I had done a full marathon earlier in the year and assumed I was prepared. I was not. The elevation does something to your lungs that flat road running simply doesn't train for. I was breathing hard within the first 20 minutes, sweating through my base layer, stopping more frequently than I wanted to admit.

The paths were rocky village trails threading through farmland and small homes. Every so often a view would open — Dronagiri appearing white and enormous through a gap in the trees — and I'd forget the burning in my legs for a moment.

Gulling Top campsite sits on terraced farmland at the valley's edge. Red tents in a neat line. We dumped our packs, had lunch, and a few of us — my Mumbai dormitory group minus one who was already napping — explored a nearby stream. Someone had a DJI Pocket 2 and spent a happy hour getting B-roll footage.

The first night in a tent at sub-zero temperatures was a genuine reckoning. The sleeping bag was warm enough horizontally, but any gap at the zip let in a cold that wasn't aggressive, just relentless. I lay considering whether I needed to go outside for the bathroom. I decided I did not. I was wrong, but I held out until morning anyway.

## Day 3 — Gulling Top to Khullara Campsite

Early mornings at a shared camp with 25 people means a queue for the pit toilets. I got up early enough to avoid it. From the tent entrance, I watched the sunrise frame itself between two peaks — the exact image from every childhood drawing of mountains, but real and enormous and making the drawing seem almost insulting in its smallness.

Today's terrain required microspikes. The forest section was dense with oak and pine, the trunks unusually wide, moss-covered. Black ice appeared on the shadowed sections of path — the deceptive kind that looks like wet rock until your foot discovers it isn't. I developed a technique: step onto the deeper snow beside the ice, use both trekking poles as anchors, redistribute weight carefully. Slow, but reliable.

A mountain dog adopted us somewhere in the first hour. No collar, no owner, seemingly no agenda other than accompanying trekkers. These dogs are apparently a standard feature of Himalayan trails — they attach to groups at the start and guide them to the next camp, then presumably return or adopt the next batch. Ours was a large, grey, entirely unhurried creature who clearly knew the route better than any of us.

The Khullara meadow arrived like a reward. White in every direction, bounded by three rising mountains, Dronagiri dominant among them. We set up camp. Shortly after, light snowfall started — the kind that falls silently and settles fast. Someone found a specific boulder nearby that, for reasons nobody could explain, had mobile signal. Calls were made. I video-called my family to show them the snow. The connection dropped three times and somehow that made it feel more real.

That evening, Ira told stories.

She talked about Hanumanji's search for the Sanjeevni herb — the accounts that place it specifically on Dronagiri, visible across the meadow from where we sat. She talked about Roopkund, the skeleton lake, and the medieval travelers who were buried by avalanche after indulging too lavishly during their pilgrimage. The mountains hold different things for different people. For the plains-dwellers in our group, these were stories. For the locals, they are ongoing.

She also talked about the belief systems still active in these villages — about specific spirits, about exorcism practices, about mountain realms that operate by different rules than the ones we brought with us. I'm a sceptic by default. I was a slightly less certain sceptic by the end of the evening.

## Day 4 — Khullara to Kuari Pass Summit and Back

Summit day. 5 AM. No full pack — just essentials. The cold at that hour was absolute.

I had assumed the breathing difficulty would ease as I adapted. It had not. The moment we started moving uphill, the altitude reclaimed its authority. We climbed through the section where trees give way entirely to snow — the landscape becoming a single white field interrupted only by footprints from previous groups. The patterns in the snow were strangely beautiful, a record of everyone who had come before.

The final approach is steep and rocky, the snow compacted and uneven. My legs were running out of messages to send by the time the gradient levelled and I realised I was at the top.

Kuari Pass sits at 12,750 feet. The summit plateau is flat enough for all 25 of us to stand comfortably. Trek leaders appeared from nowhere with hot tea. I cannot overstate how much I needed that tea.

Group photographs happened — with the red triangular temple flag, with the banner, on the rock stacks nearby. One of those photos where you balance on a summit boulder with the entire Himalayan range behind you, arms out, trying not to look as terrified as you are. I got the shot.

Bunny named the peaks visible from the top: Kamet, Mandir Parbat, Mana Parbat, Nilgiri Parbat, Ghoda Parbat, Hathi Parbat, Dronagiri, Chaukhamba — all exceeding 6,000 metres. Each name landed with a specific weight. You're standing at what feels like an enormous altitude and these peaks are still enormously taller.

The descent was treacherous. Morning sun had warmed the top layer of snow into something unreliable. Controlled slips became a technique — leaning back, poles out, letting gravity do some of the work but not all of it. On the steeper sections I sat and slid, which is efficient and undignified in equal measure.

We were back at camp by 2 PM. I ate immediately, then lay in my tent for an hour and stared at the ceiling.

Dinner was *jalebi* — the sweet, fried, syrup-soaked spiral pastry. I still have no idea how they made it happen at 11,000 feet. I didn't ask. I ate three.

## Day 5 — Khullara to Pipalkoti

The planned alternate return route was closed — recent snowfall had made it impassable. We reversed the route. I'd expected to find this disappointing. I didn't. The descending path revealed details the upward effort had made me miss: the particular orange of certain lichen on the rocks, the sound structure of the forest, the exact width of the stream at Gulling.

Confidence had accumulated somewhere over the past four days. I walked differently than I had on Day 2 — same trail, different person using it.

Near Gulling, a makeshift café materialised beside the path. They sold *Buransh* juice — made from Rhododendron flowers, pale pink, slightly tangy, reminiscent of rose sherbet but with its own specific mountain character. I bought two glasses.

The mountain dog was still with us.

We reached Tugasi, loaded into the vehicles, drove back toward Pipalkoti. On the drive, through a gap in the ridgeline, Nanda Devi appeared briefly — the second-highest peak in India, at 7,816 metres. Just for a moment, then gone behind the ridge.

The certificate ceremony was simple. Bunny quizzed us on the peak names. Most people remembered most of them. Ira gave a brief closing address: health consistency, discipline, the value of leaving your comfort zone regularly. Not phrased as motivational content — more as a practical observation from someone who has watched hundreds of people attempt something difficult and succeed at it.

I resolved to come back. I also resolved to finish the novel I'd brought and not opened once.

## What I'd Tell a First Timer

**On fitness:** a marathon does not prepare you for altitude. Do stair climbs with a pack in the weeks before. Your legs will manage; your lungs need separate training.

**On gear:** microspikes are non-negotiable for a winter trek. Borrow them if you don't want to buy. The trekking poles save your knees on descent.

**On the cold:** the sleeping bag will keep your core warm. Your face will be cold. A balaclava is not optional.

**On the mental part:** the first day is the hardest psychologically, not physically. Once you're past that first night in a tent, something settles. The mountain routine — wake, trek, eat, sleep — is clarifying in a way that's hard to explain to someone who hasn't done it.

**On why to go:** Kuari Pass is categorised as medium difficulty, which is accurate and also undersells it. The views from the summit — that specific arrangement of peaks, that specific altitude — are not available anywhere else. You cannot replicate them on a screen or in a description including this one.

Go.
